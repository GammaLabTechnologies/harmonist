# Brainstorming

**Use when:** the problem is open-ended and you need a spread of options before
committing — architecture choices, naming, product directions, ways to fix a
gnarly bug.

## Method

1. **Frame the question sharply.** One sentence: what are we generating options
   for, and what makes an option "good" here (the constraints/criteria)?
2. **Diverge first — quantity over quality.** Produce several genuinely
   different options (aim for 5+), including at least one unconventional one.
   Do NOT evaluate while generating; suspend judgment so the obvious answer
   doesn't crowd out the better one.
3. **Cluster & dedupe.** Group similar ideas; keep the strongest representative
   of each cluster.
4. **Converge with trade-offs.** For the few finalists, lay out pros/cons
   against the criteria from step 1 — honestly, including the costs of your
   preferred option. Note what would have to be true for each to win.
5. **Recommend + reversibility.** Pick one, say why, and flag whether it's a
   one-way door (hard to reverse — decide carefully) or a two-way door (cheap
   to try and undo — bias to action).

## Output

- framing: the question + the criteria for a good answer
- options: the diverged set (short, distinct, with the wild card)
- finalists: 2-4 with explicit trade-offs against the criteria
- recommendation: the pick, the rationale, and reversibility (one-way / two-way)
- open_questions: what would change the recommendation

## Guardrails

- Don't converge too early — if you only generated variations of one idea,
  diverge again.
- Be honest about the downsides of your favored option; a brainstorm that only
  argues for the foregone conclusion is theatre.
- For two-way-door decisions, prefer trying over endless debate.
